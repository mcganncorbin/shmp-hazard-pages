Tropical Systems

Tropical Systems Risk Across Maryland

Tropical systems in Maryland include hurricanes, tropical storms, and the inland remnants of coastal storms that can produce damaging winds, heavy rainfall, flooding, power outages, and transportation disruptions. While the strongest direct impacts are generally concentrated in more exposed coastal and highly developed counties, tropical systems can affect communities statewide through a combination of wind, rain, and secondary flood impacts.

Hazard

Hurricane HRCN

FEMA’s National Risk Index evaluates hurricane risk using modeled expected annual losses and exposure metrics. In Maryland, higher relative risk is concentrated in counties with large populations, significant built assets, and repeated exposure to tropical storm and hurricane impacts, including wind damage and inland flooding from storm remnants.

Key Impacts

How tropical systems affect Maryland communities

  • High winds can damage roofs, siding, trees, and overhead utilities, creating debris and power outages.
  • Heavy rainfall from tropical systems can trigger inland flooding, flash flooding, and drainage system overload.
  • Low-lying and coastal areas may also experience tidal flooding, surge-related impacts, and erosion.
  • Transportation corridors, schools, businesses, and public services can be disrupted by flooding, downed trees, and extended outages.
  • Agricultural losses can occur from crop damage, saturated fields, wind impacts, and prolonged wet conditions.

Read the full narrative text (technical detail)

Tropical Systems (Hurricane): Tropical systems risk in Maryland reflects a combination of wind exposure, inland and coastal flood potential, development intensity, and the concentration of people, buildings, infrastructure, and economic assets. Counties with relatively high risk generally combine larger populations and asset values with repeated exposure to tropical storm and hurricane impacts.

The relatively moderate tier includes a broad group of counties that still face meaningful risk from tropical systems, especially from heavy rainfall, power outages, downed trees, and transportation disruptions. Even where direct coastal exposure is lower, inland remnants of tropical systems can still produce damaging impacts.

The relatively low tier in western Maryland reflects lower modeled annualized losses compared to the rest of the state, though these counties can still experience heavy rain, isolated wind damage, and service disruption when strong storm remnants track inland.
