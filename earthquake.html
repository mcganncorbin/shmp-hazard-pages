Earthquake Risk Across Maryland

Maryland’s modeled earthquake risk is very low overall. Differences in county risk ratings are driven less by earthquake frequency and more by exposure, including population density, building concentration, and potential economic loss if an event occurs.

Earthquake Hazard

Earthquake ERQK

Although damaging earthquakes are infrequent in Maryland, even a lower-probability event can produce notable losses where people, buildings, transportation systems, and critical facilities are concentrated. Relative risk varies across counties primarily because exposure is higher in central Maryland than in more rural parts of the state.

Key Impacts

How earthquake conditions could affect Maryland communities

  • Even moderate shaking can damage older buildings, unreinforced masonry, and non-structural building components.
  • Earthquake impacts can disrupt bridges, utilities, communications networks, and water systems.
  • Repair needs and downtime can interrupt commerce, government operations, and essential public services.
  • Public safety risk tends to increase where more residents, workers, and critical facilities are concentrated in the same area.
  • Although statewide frequency is low, losses can still be significant where shaking affects densely developed counties.

Read the full narrative text (technical detail)

Earthquake: Maryland has a very low overall earthquake risk, but county differences in relative risk are influenced primarily by exposure rather than hazard frequency alone. Counties categorized as relatively moderate are concentrated in central Maryland, including Anne Arundel, Baltimore County, Baltimore City, Montgomery, and Prince George’s, where population, building inventory, and economic exposure are greater.

Counties categorized as relatively low cover much of the state and generally reflect lower modeled losses and less concentrated exposure. The very low category is concentrated in parts of Western Maryland and the Eastern Shore, where overall exposure is lower relative to central Maryland.

Data source: FEMA National Risk Index (NRI), Maryland county table. Metrics shown include Earthquake (ERQK_*) Expected Annual Loss totals and component fields, county relative risk ratings, and exposure area.
