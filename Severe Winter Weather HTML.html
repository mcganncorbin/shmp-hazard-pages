Severe Winter Weather

Severe Winter Weather Risk Across Maryland

Severe winter weather risk in Maryland includes both broader winter weather conditions and ice storm impacts. FEMA’s National Risk Index evaluates these hazards separately, and the sections below summarize statewide modeled annualized impacts, county-level relative risk patterns, accessible data tables, and tier interpretations aligned to the updated map categories.

Severe Winter Weather Sub-Hazard

Winter Weather WNTW

Winter weather risk reflects the combined impacts of snow, sleet, freezing conditions, and related disruptions to transportation, utilities, public safety, and daily operations. Relative risk is higher in counties where winter storms overlap with denser populations, infrastructure, and greater modeled annualized losses.

Key Impacts

How winter weather affects Maryland communities

  • Snow and ice reduce road traction and visibility, increasing crash risk and slowing emergency response.
  • Heavy snow and ice can damage trees and overhead lines, contributing to outages and restoration needs.
  • Public facilities, schools, and businesses may close or delay operations during significant events.
  • Winter maintenance, plowing, salting, and fleet operations increase public-sector costs and equipment strain.
  • Extended cold and storm conditions can increase demand for sheltering, warming resources, and public information.

Severe Winter Weather Sub-Hazard

Ice Storm ISTM

Ice storm risk reflects the impacts of freezing rain and ice accumulation on power systems, trees, transportation, critical facilities, and public safety. Even where overall modeled losses are lower than broad winter weather impacts, ice storms can produce severe localized disruption and prolonged outages.

Key Impacts

How ice storms affect Maryland communities

  • Freezing rain accumulates on power lines and trees, producing outages and cascading service disruptions.
  • Ice loading breaks limbs and brings down trees, blocking roads and damaging structures and vehicles.
  • Critical facilities may face increased backup power needs during prolonged outages.
  • Heating interruptions and limited mobility can elevate health and safety risks during winter conditions.
  • Even moderate ice accumulation can create significant road hazards and delay recovery operations.

FEMA-aligned framing: NRI metrics represent modeled annualized estimates derived from hazard frequency, exposure, and loss ratios. Statewide totals shown on this page use the Maryland NRI table (county-level data aggregated to state).

Winter Weather: Higher relative winter weather risk is concentrated in central Maryland, where population density and concentrations of structures increase modeled annual losses. In the county table, Cecil is categorized as relatively high along with Carroll, Frederick, and Howard, while Anne Arundel, Baltimore, Baltimore City, Harford, Montgomery, and Prince George’s are categorized as very high.

Ice Storm: Ice storm risk is categorized as relatively moderate across a set of more exposed counties in central Maryland and portions of the Eastern Shore and Southern Maryland. In the county table, Cecil is included in the relatively moderate category alongside Anne Arundel, Baltimore, Baltimore City, Frederick, Harford, Howard, Montgomery, Prince George’s, St. Mary’s, and Wicomico. Garrett County is categorized as very low.

Data source: FEMA National Risk Index (NRI) v1.20.0 (December 2025). Metrics shown include Winter Weather (WNTW_*) and Ice Storm (ISTM_*) Expected Annual Loss totals and component fields, along with county relative risk ratings.
