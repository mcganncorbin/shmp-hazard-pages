Key Impacts

How landslide conditions affect Maryland communities

  • Saturated soils and unstable slopes can fail, causing soil, rock, and debris to move downslope.
  • Roadways, rail corridors, embankments, and cut slopes can be blocked or damaged by localized slope failures.
  • Utilities, drainage infrastructure, retaining systems, and foundations near unstable slopes may require repair or stabilization.
  • Most impacts are localized, but damage can be significant where development intersects steep terrain or modified slopes.
  • Heavy rainfall and disturbed ground conditions can increase the likelihood of slope instability in susceptible areas.

FEMA-aligned framing: NRI metrics represent modeled annualized estimates derived from hazard probability, exposure, and loss ratios. The NRI evaluates soil movement under the Landslide dataset.

Soil Movement (Landslide): Landslide risk in Maryland is generally low on the FEMA relative scale, with localized areas categorized as relatively low where terrain, slope conditions, and exposure intersect. These events can occur as soil slips, debris flows, or slope failures, particularly where rainfall saturates soils or where slopes have been modified by construction and transportation corridors.

In the county table, Garrett, Allegany, Washington, Baltimore, Montgomery, Prince George’s, Calvert, and Anne Arundel are categorized as relatively low. Frederick, Carroll, Howard, Harford, Cecil, Kent, Queen Anne’s, Caroline, Talbot, Dorchester, Wicomico, Worcester, Somerset, Charles, and St. Mary’s are categorized as very low.

Data source: FEMA National Risk Index (NRI), Maryland state totals table and county relative risk categories. Metrics shown include Landslide (LNDS_*) Expected Annual Loss totals and component fields, along with county relative risk ratings.
