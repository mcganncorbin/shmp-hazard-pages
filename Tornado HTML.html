Key Impacts

How tornadoes affect Maryland communities

  • Tornadoes can cause roof and wall failures, broken windows, and severe localized damage along narrow paths.
  • Downed lines and damaged equipment can disrupt power, communications, and critical facility operations.
  • Fallen trees and wind-borne debris can block roads, delay emergency response, and increase cleanup and restoration costs.
  • Public safety risks increase where population density is high or sheltering options are limited during fast-moving severe weather.
  • Even short-duration tornado events can produce concentrated damage to homes, businesses, vehicles, and community facilities.

Read the full narrative text (technical detail)

FEMA-aligned framing: NRI metrics represent modeled annualized estimates derived from hazard probability, exposure, and loss ratios.

Tornado: The highest relative tornado risk in Maryland is concentrated in Baltimore City and Prince George’s County, where dense development and large concentrations of people and structures increase modeled annualized losses.

Baltimore, Anne Arundel, Frederick, and Montgomery counties are categorized as relatively moderate in the current NRI map pattern, reflecting substantial exposure with somewhat lower modeled relative risk than the highest category.

Much of the rest of the state is categorized as relatively low or very low. These lower relative ratings do not indicate the absence of tornado risk, but rather comparatively lower modeled losses on the NRI relative scale.
