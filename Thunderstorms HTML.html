Thunderstorm Sub-Hazard

Hail HAIL

Hail events in Maryland are generally localized, but they can still damage roofs, siding, vehicles, and agricultural resources. While statewide hail losses remain lower than other thunderstorm hazards, crop exposure and built-asset concentration can increase localized vulnerability.

Key Impacts

How hail affects Maryland communities

  • Hail can damage shingles, siding, skylights, windows, gutters, and vehicles, especially during short-duration severe storms.
  • Agricultural losses can occur when hail damages crops, orchards, and other exposed farm assets during the growing season.
  • Localized property damage can trigger repair costs, insurance claims, and temporary disruptions for homes and businesses.
  • Impacts are often concentrated along narrow storm paths, so severe losses can occur in one county while nearby areas see little damage.
  • Even where countywide risk is low, isolated large-hail events can still produce meaningful local losses.

Lightning LTNG

Lightning risk in Maryland is influenced by thunderstorm frequency, population exposure, outdoor activity, and the concentration of structures and infrastructure that can be damaged by strikes, fire, or associated outages. Key Impacts

How lightning affects Maryland communities

  • Lightning can injure or kill people exposed during outdoor work, recreation, sports, and travel.
  • Strikes can ignite structure fires and damage homes, public buildings, barns, and other facilities.
  • Power lines, transformers, and communications systems can be damaged, causing outages and service disruption.
  • Critical facilities may experience downtime when protective systems fail or backup systems are stressed.
  • Counties with larger populations and more built assets tend to show higher modeled lightning losses.

Key Impacts

How strong wind affects Maryland communities

  • Damaging gusts can bring down trees and limbs, damaging homes, vehicles, roadways, and utility lines.
  • Power outages may affect residents, businesses, schools, and critical facilities when poles, wires, and equipment fail.
  • Roofing, siding, signage, and other exposed building components can be damaged during severe thunderstorm winds.
  • Blocked roads and signal outages can disrupt commuting, freight movement, and emergency response operations.
  • Counties with dense development and extensive infrastructure generally show higher modeled strong wind losses.

Read the full narrative text (technical detail)

Hail: Hail risk in Maryland is generally lower than other thunderstorm-related hazards, but localized impacts can still be meaningful where higher-value development and agriculture are exposed. The current county risk pattern shows the highest modeled hail losses concentrated in Montgomery County, with relatively moderate losses in several central Maryland counties and lower modeled losses elsewhere.

Lightning: Lightning risk is concentrated in central Maryland where thunderstorm exposure overlaps with large populations, dense development, and extensive infrastructure. Counties in the highest tiers reflect greater modeled losses from injury risk, fire potential, and damage to electrical and communications systems.

Strong Wind: Strong wind risk is also highest across the central development corridor, where severe thunderstorm gusts can affect large numbers of people, structures, transportation assets, and utility systems. Lower-tier counties can still experience damaging wind events, but modeled annualized losses are lower on the NRI scale.
