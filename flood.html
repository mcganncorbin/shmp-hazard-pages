Flooding

Coastal and Inland Flood Risk Across Maryland

Flooding is one of Maryland’s most significant natural hazards. Risk patterns vary by geography, with tidal and storm surge impacts affecting coastal communities and rainfall-driven riverine, flash, and urban flooding affecting inland jurisdictions. The sections below summarize statewide flood risk using FEMA National Risk Index data, paired with accessible map alternatives and county tier breakouts.

Flooding Sub-Hazard

Coastal Flooding CFLD

Coastal flooding in Maryland is driven by tidal influences, storm surge, and long-term shoreline exposure along the Chesapeake Bay and Atlantic coastline. Risk is generally highest in low-lying jurisdictions where inundation affects developed waterfront areas, infrastructure, and evacuation routes.

Key Impacts

How coastal flooding affects Maryland communities

  • Repeated tidal inundation can damage roads, drainage systems, and shoreline infrastructure.
  • Storm surge and elevated water levels can threaten homes, businesses, and public facilities in low-lying areas.
  • Flooding along evacuation routes and access roads can disrupt emergency response and daily travel.
  • Saltwater intrusion and standing water can affect environmental resources, agricultural lands, and septic systems.
  • Recurring nuisance flooding can create cumulative economic losses even during less severe events.

Flooding Sub-Hazard

Inland Flooding IFLD

Inland flooding in Maryland results from rainfall-driven processes, including riverine flooding, flash flooding, and urban stormwater overflow. Risk is influenced by watershed conditions, land development patterns, impervious surfaces, and the concentration of people and assets in flood-prone corridors.

Key Impacts

How inland flooding affects Maryland communities

  • Heavy rainfall can overwhelm streams, storm drains, and culverts, especially in developed and low-lying areas.
  • Flooding can damage homes, businesses, roads, bridges, and utility infrastructure.
  • Flash flooding can create life-safety risks with little warning, particularly near road crossings and urban drainage systems.
  • Repeated inland flooding can disrupt schools, emergency services, and local commerce.
  • Flood-prone development patterns can increase long-term recovery costs and cumulative property losses.

Read the full narrative text (technical detail)

Coastal Flooding: Coastal flooding risk in Maryland is associated with tidal flooding, storm surge, and shoreline exposure along the Chesapeake Bay and Atlantic coast. Relative risk is shaped by the presence of low-lying developed areas, exposure of structures and infrastructure, and the frequency of water level impacts.

Inland Flooding: Inland flooding risk is associated with riverine systems, flash flooding, and rainfall-driven stormwater impacts. Relative risk is generally highest in areas where flood exposure overlaps with dense development, transportation networks, and vulnerable built assets.
