Extreme Temperatures

Cold Wave and Heat Wave Risk Across Maryland

Extreme temperature hazards in Maryland include both dangerous cold and dangerous heat. Although these hazards affect people statewide, relative risk varies by county based on population exposure, infrastructure, development patterns, and modeled annualized losses. The sections below summarize statewide risk patterns for Cold Wave and Heat Wave using FEMA National Risk Index data, paired with accessible map alternatives and county tier breakouts.

Extreme Temperatures Sub-Hazard

Cold Wave CWAV

Cold wave risk reflects the potential impacts of extreme cold on people, buildings, agriculture, and infrastructure. Risk tends to increase where cold exposure overlaps with larger populations, higher concentrations of buildings and services, and greater system sensitivity to freezing conditions.

Key Impacts

How cold wave conditions affect Maryland communities

  • Extreme cold can increase the risk of hypothermia, frostbite, and other serious health impacts, especially for vulnerable populations.
  • Prolonged freezing conditions can increase heating demand and amplify the consequences of electric or fuel service disruptions.
  • Cold weather can damage water systems, burst pipes, and strain transportation infrastructure and maintenance operations.
  • Schools, businesses, and public services may face closures or delayed operations during severe cold events.
  • Agricultural operations and outdoor workers can face additional losses or disruption during extreme cold periods.

Extreme Temperatures Sub-Hazard

Heat Wave HWAV

Heat wave risk reflects the public health and economic consequences of prolonged high temperatures. Risk is often shaped by population density, urban heat island effects, limited access to cooling, and the concentration of people, buildings, and services exposed to dangerous heat conditions.

Key Impacts

How heat wave conditions affect Maryland communities

  • Extreme heat increases the risk of heat exhaustion, heat stroke, dehydration, and heat-related mortality.
  • Urban and suburban areas with more impervious surfaces can experience intensified heat from urban heat island effects.
  • High temperatures can drive peak electricity demand for cooling and increase the consequences of outages.
  • Outdoor work, recreation, and public events may be disrupted during prolonged heat episodes.
  • Public health systems, cooling centers, and social services can face additional strain during dangerous heat events.

In the county table, Baltimore, Baltimore City, Montgomery, and Prince George’s are categorized as relatively high.
