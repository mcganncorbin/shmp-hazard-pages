Wildfire Risk Across Maryland

Wildfire risk in Maryland is generally lower than many other natural hazards, but it still affects parts of the state through vegetation fires, smoke, localized threats to structures, and potential impacts to utilities, transportation, and emergency response access. The information below summarizes statewide FEMA National Risk Index metrics and the county-level relative risk pattern shown in the wildfire map.

Hazard

Wildfire WFIR

FEMA’s National Risk Index evaluates wildfire risk using modeled expected annual losses and exposure metrics. In Maryland, wildfire risk is concentrated more in counties where vegetation, open land, and rural development patterns create greater opportunity for localized fire spread, while many more urbanized counties remain in the very low tier.

Key Impacts

How wildfire affects Maryland communities

  • Wildfires can threaten homes, outbuildings, and infrastructure where development is near wooded or brush-covered areas.
  • Smoke can reduce air quality, affect public health, and disrupt outdoor work, recreation, and travel.
  • Fires can damage utility corridors, fencing, natural resources, and transportation access routes.
  • Suppression needs can strain emergency response resources, especially during dry and windy conditions.
  • Even where statewide risk is relatively low, localized wildland-urban interface areas can still experience meaningful impacts.

Read the full narrative text (technical detail)

Wildfire: Wildfire risk in Maryland remains comparatively low statewide on the FEMA National Risk Index scale, with counties falling into either relatively low or very low categories in the current map pattern. The relatively low tier is concentrated in parts of Southern Maryland and the Eastern Shore, where vegetation, rural land cover, and development patterns can create greater wildfire exposure than in more urbanized counties.

Counties in the very low tier still face the possibility of brush fires, woods fires, and localized smoke impacts, but modeled annualized losses are lower than in the relatively low counties. Even so, periods of drought, high winds, and dry fuels can still increase wildfire potential in localized areas.
